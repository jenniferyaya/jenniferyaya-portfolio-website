Clara

Planning trips with friends often sounds fun at the start, but the reality quickly becomes messy: scattered conversations, uneven workloads, lost decisions, and a single person carrying most of the burden.

Clara named from the Latin word for bright and transparent is an iOS mobile app that aims to reduce that cognitive load and create a planning experience that feels calm, organized, and genuinely collaborative.

This project followed a full human-centered design process, from foundational research through low and mid-fidelity prototyping and usability testing. Clara’s mission is simple: make planning together feel just as enjoyable as the trip itself.

Problem

Group trips often fall apart not because people don’t care, but because planning is chaotic. Conversations happen across multiple apps. Tasks are forgotten. One person ends up being the unofficial planner. Decisions get buried in threads. Money and responsibility feel awkward to navigate.

My research surfaced a core insight: People want group trips to feel shared, fair, and connected, but the existing planning tools make collaboration harder, not easier.

Clara is designed to solve this by giving groups a single, clear place to plan together.

Research & Insights

I conducted surveys and interviews with six participants who frequently plan trips with friends or partners. Patterns quickly emerged:

  • Uneven workload: One person inevitably becomes the planner.
  • Decision fatigue: Too many back-and-forth messages.
  • Unclear ownership: Tasks slip through cracks because responsibility isn’t visible.
  • Fragmented tools: People bounce between chats, notes, spreadsheets, and booking apps.
  • Stress and anxiety: Group trips should be joyful, but planning often causes friction.

Key insight: Group travelers need a clear, shared way to assign responsibilities and see progress so that planning feels fair and balanced. This insight shaped all decisions that followed.

How Might We…

How might we help travel groups share planning responsibilities so that organizing a trip feels fair, collaborative, and stress-free?

This HMW anchored every design choice.

Turning Insight Into Functionality

I created 25 user stories, then grouped them into epics related to shared planning, communication, budgeting, and emotional engagement. The epic with the highest user value and strongest link to the problem space was:

Shared Planning & Decision-Making

This became the focus of my prototype. From there, I built a detailed task flow showing how a user (Maya Chen, my persona) creates, assigns, and completes trip-planning tasks.

Meet Maya, a social and organized planner who often ends up managing every detail. Her needs and pain points shaped the direction of the solution.

Maya’s experience map reveals the highs and lows of planning with others—from initial excitement to decision fatigue. These moments showed where Clara could offer clarity and balance.

Exploring the Interface

I gathered UI inspiration focused on:

  • clean task management patterns,
  • calm visual hierarchy.

Through exploratory sketches, I tested different ways to visualize responsibilities, progress, and group contribution. The strongest ideas were refined into solution sketches and grayscale wireframes.

Prototype (V1)

The first interactive prototype focused on the core task flow:

  • viewing the trip dashboard
  • creating a new task.

The design leaned into clarity,simple patterns, clear labels, and enough space around content so users wouldn’t feel overwhelmed.

Usability Testing

I tested the V1 prototype with five users who had experience planning trips with others. What worked:

  • clear structure and navigation,
  • smooth task-creation flow.

Where users hesitated:

  • uncertainty around the copy, uncertainity about the differences between terms,
  • uncertainty and lack of a clear dashboard or home state.
  • difficulty understanding category vs. task actions.

Prioritization & Iteration

Using a design prioritization matrix, I focused on high-impact improvements:

  • Clarifying category labels to reduce confusion between Current, Planning, and Drafts.
  • Strengthening the dashboard hierarchy so users clearly understand where they are in the app.
  • Improving the CTA hierarchy, especially the “Start planning a trip” button, so it stands out at the right times.
  • Adjusting icon meanings, such as the suitcase icon, to better match user expectations for Home.
  • Making task actions more visible, including clearer entry points for adding tasks at the category level.

These refinements informed Prototype V2.

These early sketches show how users might manage tasks and responsibilities, followed by the live prototype that introduces Clara’s simple, supportive onboarding experience.

Key Learnings

Working through this project taught me how much clarity and intention matter in UX design. Each stage revealed something new about how people think, decide, and interact with digital tools.

Research is not about volume, it’s about clarity: I learned that good research is not measured by how many responses you collect, but by how well you can see the patterns within them. Speaking with six participants was enough to uncover the core issue in group travel planning: one person often carries most of the work, and the tools we use make that imbalance worse.

One strong insight will guide the entire project: The main insight that shaped this work was simple but powerful: people want a fair and balanced way to plan trips together, but current tools do not support shared responsibility. This insight influenced every step of the design process and kept the solution grounded in real user needs.

Words matter just as much as visuals: During usability testing, many points of confusion came from unclear labels and instructions. This showed me that microcopy is a design tool. If the words are unclear, the experience is unclear.

This project helped me grow as a designer by showing me the value of focusing on people, simplifying ideas, and communicating clearly. It confirmed that thoughtful UX design relies on both research and language, and that even small improvements can make the experience feel easier and more balanced.

Next Steps
