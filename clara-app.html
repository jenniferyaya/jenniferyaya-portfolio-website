Clara

Planning a trip with friends usually starts with excitement, but that energy fades fast once the logistics begin. Details scatter across group chats, decisions get buried, budgets feel unclear, and one person,often the most organized friend quietly becomes the planner. What should feel fun starts to feel heavy.

Clara was designed to ease that weight.

Named after the Latin word for bright and clear, Clara is an iOS app that helps groups plan trips in a way that feels organized, fair, and genuinely collaborative. Instead of relying on separate chats, documents, spreadsheets, and reminders, Clara brings everything together so responsibilities are shared and progress is visible.

This case study walks through the full design process, from early research to iterative prototyping. The goal: create a calmer, more balanced experience for group travelers.

Problem

Groups don’t struggle because they don’t care, they struggle because planning is messy. Conversations happen across multiple apps. Tasks get forgotten. Budgets feel unclear. And one person inevitably becomes the unofficial planner, shouldering most of the effort.

Through research, I found a deeper issue: People want group trips to feel shared, fair, and connected, but the tools they use actually make collaboration harder.

Participants described:

  • jumping between chats, notes, and spreadsheets
  • losing track of decisions
  • waiting on others to respond
  • feeling frustrated by uneven workload
  • becoming anxious when plans shifted

Clara aims to solve this by giving groups a single, shared space to plan—reducing cognitive load and making collaboration feel natural.

Research & Insights

To understand group travel behaviors, I surveyed and interviewed six people who frequently plan trips with partners or friends. Patterns quickly emerged:

  • One person takes the lead. Every participant described becoming the planner without meaning to.
  • Decisions are scattered. Important details get buried in threads or forgotten.
  • Ownership is unclear. People assume someone else will handle tasks.
  • Tools are fragmented. Users bounce between chats, notes, spreadsheets, and booking sites.
  • Planning causes stress. What should be exciting becomes a source of tension.

One insight guided everything that followed: Group travelers need a clear, shared way to assign responsibilities and see progress so that planning feels fair and balanced. This insight became the foundation of Clara’s feature direction.

How Might We…

How might we help travel groups share planning responsibilities so that organizing a trip feels fair, collaborative, and stress-free?

This guiding question shaped every design decision.

Turning Insight Into Functionality

I translated research into 25 user stories, grouped into themes like shared planning, communication, budgeting, and emotional engagement. The epic with the highest value for both clarity and fairness was:

Shared Planning & Decision-Making

This epic centered on:

  • assigning tasks to specific people
  • seeing progress at a glance
  • keeping responsibilities visible
  • making collaboration simple rather than stressful

From here, I built a detailed task flow showing how a user like Maya (my persona) creates, assigns, and completes trip-planning tasks.

Meet Maya, a social and organized planner created from interviews to represent the friend who often becomes the default trip planner.

Maya’s experience map reveals the highs and lows of planning with others, from initial excitement to decision fatigue. These moments showed where Clara could offer clarity and balance.

Exploring the Interface

I gathered mobile UI inspiration focused on:

  • clean task management patterns
  • calm visual hierarchy
  • clear ownership indicators
  • lightweight interaction flows

Exploratory sketches tested different structures for task lists, ownership tags, progress indicators, and creation flows. I aimed to design a workspace that felt light and easy to navigate, especially during busy planning moments.

The strongest sketches were refined into solution wireframes.

Prototype (V1)

The first interactive prototype focused on the core user flow:

  • viewing the group’s trip dashboard
  • creating a new task

The design leaned into:

  • simple patterns
  • clear labels
  • open space around content
  • predictable navigation

This helped reduce cognitive load and set a calm foundation.

Usability Testing

I tested the V1 prototype with five users who had planned trips with others.

What worked well:

  • The structure felt clean and easy to scan
  • The task-creation flow felt intuitive
  • Users appreciated how responsibilities were clearly assigned

Where users hesitated:

  • Unclear distinctions between categories (Drafts vs. Planning)
  • Confusion around icons (what the suitcase icon meant).
  • Missing cues for how to add tasks at the category level
  • Lack of a clear home state

These insights helped me refine the hierarchy and language.

Prioritization & Iteration

Using a design prioritization matrix, I focused on improvements with the highest user impact:

  • Clarifying category labels to reduce confusion
  • Strengthening the dashboard hierarchy to show where users are
  • Improving the CTA hierarchy, so key actions are easier to spot
  • Adjusting iconography to better match user expectations
  • Making task actions more visible so users know where to begin

These refinements shaped Prototype V2, which now feels more intuitive and aligned with the user’s mental model.

Early sketches explored different ways users could manage tasks, followed by a live prototype that brings Clara to life.

Key Learnings

Working through this project taught me how much clarity and intention matter in UX design. Each stage revealed something new about how people think, decide, and interact with digital tools.

Research is not about volume, it’s about clarity: I learned that good research is not measured by how many responses you collect, but by how well you can see the patterns within them. Speaking with six participants was enough to uncover the core issue in group travel planning: one person often carries most of the work, and the tools we use make that imbalance worse.

One strong insight will guide the entire project: The main insight that shaped this work was simple but powerful: people want a fair and balanced way to plan trips together, but current tools do not support shared responsibility. This insight influenced every step of the design process and kept the solution grounded in real user needs.

Words matter just as much as visuals: During usability testing, many points of confusion came from unclear labels and instructions. This showed me that microcopy is a design tool. If the words are unclear, the experience is unclear.

This project helped me grow as a designer by showing me the value of focusing on people, simplifying ideas, and communicating clearly. It confirmed that thoughtful UX design relies on both research and language, and that even small improvements can make the experience feel easier and more balanced.
