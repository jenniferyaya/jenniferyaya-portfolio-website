The Mirror

AI felt less like a shortcut and more like a mirror — one that exposes the difference between liking something and standing behind it.

Working with AI on a project like Conserva, where image generation became part of the visual system, made that distinction sharp and immediate. The tool could produce something that looked good instantly. Whether it was right — whether it was mine — was a different question entirely. One that required slowing down, not speeding up.

Judgment as Work

The future of design will include tools that generate faster than we can imagine. But speed doesn't replace authorship. If anything, it demands more of it.

When friction disappears, judgment becomes the work. And judgment is a quiet, patient thing. I've come to think of it as almost a physical act. Small turns. Subtle corrections. The slow narrowing of focus until the image sharpens into something that feels not chosen but inevitable — like a word you searched for all day that arrives, finally, in the dark just before sleep.

The Capacity to See

"The foundation of morality is the ability to really see — to attend to what is actually there rather than what we wish were there."

— Iris Murdoch

I read that years ago and felt it land somewhere specific, the way true things do. I think it's also the foundation of craft. The capacity to see clearly, choose honestly, and resist what merely looks good.

AI doesn't threaten that capacity. But it does nothing to cultivate it, either. That work remains stubbornly, beautifully human.

The Real Question

So the real question isn't whether AI will replace designers. It's quieter than that — almost intimate. The kind of question you sit with rather than answer.

What does it mean to make something, when making becomes effortless? What do we owe the work, when the work no longer costs us anything? I don't have answers. But I think the asking matters — and I think designers are better placed than most to do it honestly.

Structure doesn't limit creativity.
And authorship doesn't disappear when the tools get easier.
It just gets harder to fake.
