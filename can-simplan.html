CAN-SIMPLAN

An AI-assisted zoning pre-screen tool that helps municipal planners evaluate development proposals faster while keeping every decision traceable, defensible, and human.

Role

Sole UX Designer

Team

Cross-disciplinary sprint team

Duration

4-day design sprint

Outcome

Prototype selected for stakeholder presentation to the City of Mississauga

The Problem

Planners don't need faster AI. They need AI they can defend.

Municipal planners evaluate complex development proposals under intense political and public scrutiny. A rejected proposal or an approved one that later fails — both carry professional and civic consequences. The planner's name is on the decision.

Existing tools can summarize data and flag anomalies. What they can't do is explain themselves. When AI produces a recommendation without showing its work, it puts the planner in an impossible position: responsible for a decision they can't fully trace.

The model might be accurate but accuracy isn't enough. I need to be able to stand up in a council meeting and explain why.

— Municipal planner, research insight

Design challenge: How might we design an AI planning tool that accelerates proposal review while ensuring every output a planner acts on is something they can explain, trace, and stand behind?

Key Insight

The AI isn't the problem. The opacity is.

Early research conversations with planners revealed a consistent pattern: the barrier to AI adoption wasn't skepticism about the model's accuracy. It was the inability to explain any individual output to a colleague, a councillor, or a community member asking hard questions.

This reframed the design problem entirely. The goal wasn't to make AI smarter or faster. It was to make it legible — to surface the reasoning behind every score, flag every assumption the model made, and keep a human hand visible at every step of the workflow.

The original framing

How do we speed up the proposal review process with AI?

The reframe

How do we make AI-assisted decisions something a planner can defend?

Product Strategy

Not a decision-maker. A structured oversight layer.

CAN-SIMPLAN is designed around one principle: the AI surfaces analysis, the planner makes the call. Every design decision was tested against that constraint. If it gave the AI more authority than the planner, it was cut.

I designed the end-to-end interface, from login to audit trail, so that every output the planner sees is something they can explain, trace, and act on with confidence.

Design Decisions

Four patterns that make accountability tangible

01

Blocking flags enforce engagement

When the simulation raises a concern, the planner cannot move forward without writing a rationale. The workflow literally stops. Engagement cannot be optional in a governance context.

Tradeoff This adds friction to every flagged case. That friction is the point — it's what transforms a checkbox into a considered judgment.
02

Confidence lives in the header, not a technical panel

Model confidence score, version, and last-run timestamp surface at the top of every proposal view. Planners need to calibrate their trust in the output before engaging with it — not after.

Tradeoff Surfacing uncertainty feels counterintuitive for an AI product. It's also the only honest approach — and what distinguishes a governance tool from a consumer product.
03

Plain language over technical accuracy

Every simulation flag includes a plain-language explanation written for planners, not data scientists. The planner's job is judgment. The interface's job is translation.

Tradeoff Simplifying technical output risks losing precision. The decision was to prioritize usability for the person responsible — and let the full technical detail live one level deeper for those who need it.
04

The audit trail belongs in the primary workflow

Every override is logged, timestamped, and immutable — inside the proposal view, not buried in an admin panel. When accountability is hidden, it becomes optional.

Tradeoff Embedding the audit trail in the main view adds visual weight to an already dense workspace. The alternative — a secondary panel no one opens — would defeat the purpose entirely.

Outcome

A decision-support tool designed not to replace planners, but to make every decision they make easier to explain.

The prototype was completed in four days as part of a cross-disciplinary sprint team and selected for presentation to the City of Mississauga. The end-to-end interface covers login through audit trail, with every screen designed around the constraint that AI analysis must pass through a human review checkpoint before the workflow can advance.

The core insight, that the barrier to AI adoption in civic contexts isn't accuracy, it's legibility, shaped every design decision from the proposal dashboard to the flag override flow.

Reflection

Designing for governance changed how I think about friction

The default instinct in UX is to remove friction. This project pushed me to question that. In high-accountability contexts, some friction is protective, it's the mechanism by which a workflow enforces engagement rather than just recording it.

Working at the boundary between simulation logic and human decision-making also sharpened a skill I hadn't expected to develop: translating in both directions, from technical constraints into interface clarity, and from planner needs back into system requirements.

  • Usability testing on the flag-override flows: does mandatory rationale actually change planner behaviour, or create workarounds?
  • A full accessibility audit: civic tools must meet the highest accessibility standards without exception
  • The post-approval feedback loop: how real outcomes recalibrate the model over time, and how that recalibration is communicated to planners
  • Research into how different municipalities weight domain scores differently, and whether the interface can adapt to those preferences

The most interesting design question CAN-SIMPLAN raises isn't 'how do we make AI faster?', it's 'how do we make accountability a feature, not a constraint?'
